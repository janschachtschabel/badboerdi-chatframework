# 3. The Creative Commons Licencing Scheme

**Autor**: Dr. Till Kreutzer | **Quelle**: Open Content Practical Guide | **Lizenz**: CC BY 4.0

CC is by far the most widespread Open Content licencing model. Its popularity and widespread use means CC can nowadays be considered **de facto as the standard** for Open Content licencing.

---

## 3.1 Overview of the Six CC Licence Types

CC provides a set of **six licences and two public domain tools**. Each licence contains one or more of four basic elements (the „licence features") which are illustrated by abbreviations and pictograms:

| Feature | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **BY** | Attribution — the obligation to credit the author and other parties designated for attribution |
| **NC** | NonCommercial — commercial use is excluded from the licence grant |
| **ND** | NoDerivatives — only verbatim copies of the work can be shared |
| **SA** | ShareAlike — the work can be modified and modified versions can be published but only under the original or a compatible licence |

These four features form the basis of a fixed set of six CC licences:

### a) CC BY (Attribution)

The most permissive licence. Grants unrestricted, irrevocable, royalty-free, worldwide, indefinite rights to use the work in any way, by any user and for any purpose. The only requirement is that the user credits the author and retains copyright and licence notices.

According to section 3a of the legal code, the following obligations must be met:

1. The author and other parties designated to receive attribution must be named in the manner requested by the licenser as long as the requested form is reasonable.
2. Copyright notices, a reference to the CC licence, a notice that refers to the disclaimer of warranty and liability and a link to the original source must be retained.
3. If the work is shared in an adapted version, it must be indicated that it is a modified version.
4. If the licenser requests to remove any of the information referred to in paragraph 2, the user has to do so as long as it is reasonable.
5. The licensee must not create the impression that their use is endorsed by the licenser.

### b) CC BY-SA (Attribution-ShareAlike)

As the general licence of **Wikipedia**, CC BY-SA is one of the most important and widespread CC licences. Licensers who would like their content to be uploaded onto Wikipedia, or to combine it with Wikipedia content, are advised to use CC BY-SA.

The only difference between CC BY-SA and CC BY is the ShareAlike clause: Adapted versions must be shared under CC BY-SA or a compatible licence.

### c) CC BY-ND (Attribution-NoDerivatives)

Does not permit adaptations of the work. To protect its integrity, **only verbatim copies** may be distributed and shared. The NoDerivatives restriction can lead to significant problems with the combination of different content, e.g. in remixing, sampling or joined publications.

### d) CC BY-NC (Attribution-NonCommercial)

Reserves the right to use the content commercially. A user is **not allowed to reproduce the work or create derivatives if their purpose is to realise a commercial gain**.

### e) CC BY-NC-SA (Attribution-NonCommercial-ShareAlike)

Combines the NonCommercial and ShareAlike features. Used, e.g. by the **MIT Open Courseware Project (OCW)**.

### f) CC BY-NC-ND (Attribution-NonCommercial-NoDerivatives)

The **most restrictive** CC licence. Neither modifications nor commercial uses are permitted.

## 3.2 CC Public Domain Tools

Unlike the six licences, these tools are for works that are **not (or no longer)** under copyright protection.

### a) CC0 (No Rights Reserved)

A tool to deliberately dedicate copyright-protected works to the public domain — a **waiver of rights**. Once in effect, a work belongs to the public domain and can be used without restrictions or obligations.

Designed as a **three-tier instrument** for worldwide validity:
1. **Waiver** — giving up ownership (works in jurisdictions that allow waiving)
2. **Fallback licence** — CC-BY-like without the attribution requirement
3. **Non-assertion pledge** — a legally binding promise not to enforce rights even if a waiver is invalid

> In author's-rights regimes (Germany, France, Austria), rights cannot be waived completely. The fallback options ensure CC0 still works there.

### b) Public Domain Mark (No Known Copyright)

Not a declaration but a **label** for works already in the public domain, e.g. because the term of protection has expired (in Europe: 70 years after the author's death). Before applying, a thorough inquiry about the legal status is required — the Europeana Public Domain Calculator can help.

## 3.3 Generic and Ported Licence Versions

The current version, **CCPL4**, was published on November 26, 2013. CCPL3 and CCPL4 differ in subtle but important ways.

### Porting

In 2003, CC launched „Creative Commons International" — a project to adapt CC licences linguistically **and legally** to specific jurisdictions. CCPL3 was ported into more than 60 jurisdictions.

For **CCPL4, CC is discouraging ports**. The current FAQ states:

> „We recommend that you use a version 4.0 international license. This is the most up-to-date version, drafted after broad consultation with our global network of affiliates, and it has been written to be internationally valid. There are currently no ports of 4.0, and it is planned that few, if any, will be created."

### Ported vs. Unported: Considerations

| Aspekt | Ported version | Unported / International |
|---|---|---|
| **Language** | Native language of jurisdiction | International, translations available |
| **Choice of law** | Yes, designates local law | No choice-of-law clause |
| **Best for** | National use within one jurisdiction | Online content, multi-national projects |
| **Example problem** | French port: Russian licensee can't easily read/interpret French legal terms | Applicable law determined by private international law rules |

For multi-national projects (e.g. Wikipedia), the international/unported approach is preferable.

## 3.4 Licence Conditions, User Obligations and Restrictions for All CC Licences

### a) Licence Grant

Section 2a of the legal code grants a **non-exclusive, irrevocable, royalty-free and worldwide licence** to share and copy the material.

All CC licences are **non-sublicensable** — users cannot grant rights to other users. This prevents complex licence chains.

### b) Licence Conclusion — When the Obligations Apply

Section 2.a.2 of the legal code states:
> „Exceptions and Limitations. For the avoidance of doubt, where Exceptions and Limitations apply to Your use, this Public License does not apply, and You do not need to comply with its terms and conditions."

**Key principle**: Uses that do not require a licence (statutory freedoms like quotation, private copying) do **not** trigger the licence obligations. The licence only comes into effect for **sharing** — conveying the material to members of the public.

#### The Term "Public"

Simply put, „sharing" means conveying the material to members of the public. The importance of the differentiation:

**Example 1** — Facebook user posts someone else's ND-photo (modified) to her wall, visible only to her direct contacts. Public sharing? Answer depends on interpretation — if the contacts are considered a public group, she violates the ND restriction.

**Example 2** — Company produces a brochure with modified ND photos, distributed only within the company group. Internal use or public distribution?

The **European Court of Justice** has established basic interpretation rules:

- Public means „making a work perceptible in any appropriate manner to persons in general, i.e. not restricted to specific individuals belonging to a private group"
- Public implies a **fairly large number** of persons (significant groups can also be reached in succession)
- Relevant whether the user profits monetarily
- A „new public" is required for online works
- Hyperlinks to works already made available online do **not** constitute a new communication to the public

**Safe rules of thumb**:
- Private sphere (personal relationships) = always non-public
- Online use targeting a general public = always sharing under CC
- Between separate companies = usually public distribution
- Within one company in-house = probably not sharing

### c) Attribution

The BY feature is in **all six CC licences**. Crediting is the main reward for the Open Content publisher. Rules (section 3.a.1):

- **Reasonable manner** — user can comply via any reasonable method based on medium and context
- **Link to the source** — hyperlink to the licenced material must be retained (also in offline publications — e.g. print the full Flickr URI)
- **Keep copyright/licence notices** — including the link to the licence text and warranty disclaimer
- **Indicate modifications** — to protect author's reputation and maintain version history
- **CCPL4 change**: No longer mandatory to name the work's title (still recommended)

### d) Database Rights

CCPL4 licences apply to **„copyright and/or similar rights closely related to copyright, including, without limitation, performance, broadcast, sound recording, and sui generis database rights"**.

The sui generis database right is a **European peculiarity** (introduced 1996 by the Database Directive, doesn't exist in the US). Section 4 of the legal code clarifies that the licence grant covers database rights. If a database is NC-licensed, reuse/sharing is only permitted for non-commercial purposes; if SA-licensed, any own database including a substantial part must be licensed under the same or compatible licence.

### e) Patents and Trademark Rights

Section 2.b.2: Patents and trademark rights **cannot be licenced under the CC licences**. A trademark associated with the work can only be used to share said work — not to promote the licensee's own works.

### f) Moral Rights, Privacy and Personal Rights

**Moral rights** protect the personal relationship between an author and their work (right of integrity, attribution right, first-publication right).

CCPL4 introduces a uniform approach:

> „The 4.0 licence suite uniformly and explicitly waives moral rights held by the licensor where possible to the limited extent necessary to enable reuse of the content in the manner intended by the license. Publicity, privacy, and personality rights held by the licensor are expressly waived to the same limited extent."

**Important**: If a CC-licensed photo **violates third parties' personal rights** (e.g. depicts individuals without consent), both the licenser and any downstream licensee can be held liable — regardless of knowledge.

### g) Disclaimer of Warranties and Limitation of Liability

The work is shared **„as-is"** — the licenser is not liable for damages. Under European tort law, full exclusion is not always possible. Section 4.c contains a severability clause reducing liability to the lowest level permitted by applicable law.

> Even if the liability clause were invalid, the level of liability for shared-without-compensation material is generally very low under most jurisdictions (in Germany: equivalent to the liability for gifts — the lowest possible).

### h) Prohibition of Technological Protection Measures (TPM)

Section 2.a.4: TPM protection shall **not be effective** for CC-licensed works. Any licensee may circumvent effective TPMs to use the work according to the licence terms — an explicit override of Article 6 of the EU Copyright Directive.

### i) Licence Term and Termination

- **Perpetual** (section 6.a) — applies until all related rights expire
- **Irrevocable** (section 2.a.1) — licenser cannot actively terminate
- **Automatic termination** on breach — uses after violation are copyright infringements
- **Reinstatement** (CCPL4, new):
  1. Automatic if infringement is cured within 30 days of discovery
  2. Express reinstatement by the licenser
  > User remains liable for non-compliant uses conducted before reinstatement

## 3.5 Additional Licence-Specific Restrictions and Obligations

### a) NC — NonCommercial

**Section 1i of the legal code**:
> „NonCommercial means not primarily intended for or directed towards commercial advantage or monetary compensation. ... the exchange of the Licensed Material for other material ... by digital file-sharing or similar means is NonCommercial provided there is no payment of monetary compensation in connection with the exchange."

This leaves **a lot of room for interpretation**.

#### Who Can Use NC Content in What Use Case?

| Use Case | Company | Public Inst. | Non-profit NGO | Freelancer | Private Person |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sell hardcopies | No | No | No | No | No |
| License content against payment | No | No | No | No | No |
| Use for advertising | No | No | No | No | No |
| Use to make money | No | No | No | No | No |
| Use for the job | n.a. | n.a. | n.a. | No | n.a. |
| Use on website with ads (hosting cost) | No | Yes | Yes | No | Yes |
| Use on platform with ads (by platform) | No | Yes | Yes | No | Yes |
| Use for in-house education | No | Yes | Yes | No | n.a. |
| Use for private entertainment | n.a. | n.a. | n.a. | n.a. | Yes |
| Use to inform customers/audience | No | Yes | Yes | No | Yes |
| Use in tuition-free educational courses | No | Yes | Yes | No | Yes |
| Use in tuition-based educational courses | No | No | No | No | No |
| Use for corporate-funded research | No | No | No | No | n.a. |
| Use for tax-funded research | No | Yes | Yes | No | n.a. |
| In-house corporate research | No | n.a. | n.a. | No | n.a. |

#### Advantages and Disadvantages of NC

> „An objective evaluation leads to the conclusion that the disadvantages outweigh the benefits for both creators and users in the great majority of cases."

The NC restriction affects:
- **Educational uses** — question whether NC can be used in tuition-based courses is highly disputed
- **Scientific research** — use in public-private-partnerships is arguable
- **Private websites with ads** to recover hosting costs — also arguable
- **Wikipedia integration** — NC content **cannot** be integrated (Wikipedia uses CC BY-SA)

NC licences are generally only advantageous for **professional publishers** who can deploy complex marketing strategies and pursue licence violators. Some commentators argue **„CC SA is the better NC"** — SA doesn't restrict commercial use but requires commercial distributors to negotiate individual terms anyway.

### b) ND — NoDerivatives

Intent and purpose: **protect the integrity of the work**.

#### What is an "Adaptation"?

Section 1a of the legal code:
> „Adapted Material means material subject to Copyright and Similar Rights that is derived from or based upon the Licensed Material and in which the Licensed Material is translated, altered, arranged, transformed, or otherwise modified in a manner requiring permission under the Copyright and Similar Rights held by the Licensor. For purposes of this Public License, where the Licensed Material is a musical work, performance, or sound recording, Adapted Material is always produced where the Licensed Material is synched in timed relation with a moving image."

**Not an adaptation**: format shifting, digitization (mere technical modifications).

**Indisputably adaptations**: translations, making a film out of a novel, synching music with video.

#### Adaptation vs. Collection — The Key Distinction

> **Rule of thumb**: Every time existing material is merged into a larger work which has a character of its own, the works are adapted. The more the individual works are used „as-is" and „stand-alone" (i.e. only grouped), the less likely the combination will be considered adapted material.

| Use Case | Permitted under ND? |
|---|---|
| Mashup video | No |
| Image or text in newspaper or journal | Yes |
| Music remix | No |
| Sampling | No |
| Image or text on website, blog or social media | Yes |
| Translation | No |
| Music synching | No |
| Screen adaptation of a novel | No |
| Images in catalogue | Yes |
| Article in text collection | Yes |
| Image collage | Depends (generally No) |
| Parody | Depends on the jurisdiction |
| "Kitchen-video" with background music | No |
| Documentary film integrating sound footage | No |

#### Advantages and Disadvantages of ND

ND licences are generally **not suitable for OER** (Open Educational Resources) — educational resources need to be modified and translated to serve different target groups.

Reasonable ND use cases:
- **Certified information** requiring regulation (technical standards, legal norms)
- **Business models** reserving customization rights

### c) SA — ShareAlike

Two CC licences contain ShareAlike: CC BY-SA and CC BY-NC-SA.

**Key rule**: Adapted material can only be published under the **same or a compatible licence**.

#### When Does SA Apply?

Only when: a) the material is adapted AND b) it is shared.

> **Important**: SA does **NOT oblige anybody to share** adapted material. The adapter can keep modifications private. SA is a rule on „how to share", not "whether to share".

#### Three Options for the Adapter's Licence

1. The **same CC SA licence** as the original (or any later version)
2. A **CC licence with the same elements** (e.g. a ported version — `CC BY-SA 3.0 Germany` for originally `CC BY-SA 3.0 Unported`)
3. A **BY-SA "compatible licence"** approved by CC (as of today: no licence has been approved — this option is obsolete)

### The Licence Compatibility Problem

> „Licence incompatibilities are a big problem for free culture."

A licence incompatibility occurs when the user can only comply with one of two conflicting licence obligations. Out of 64 possible ways to combine CC-licensed works in a remix/mashup, **32 are not permitted**.

**Simple pattern**: The more restrictive the licence, the fewer combination options. NC material cannot be mixed into a CC-BY-licensed remix. SA material can only combine with content whose licence allows re-licensing under the SA licence.

#### SA as "The Better NC"?

Some commentators argue CC BY-SA is preferable to NC:
- **Allows** free commercial use in principle
- **But** commercial users reluctant to adopt SA content because of the viral copyleft effect
- Need for individual negotiations arises anyway
- Protects the „creative commons" from monopolization

**Bottom line**: In the end, the licenser has to balance two motivations:
1. **Ensure openness** of the material → CC BY-SA
2. **Encourage maximum interest** in use → CC BY
